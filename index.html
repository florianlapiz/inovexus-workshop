Problem Space Before Outbound
GTM Strategist Framework
8 min

Before writing a single outbound email, you need to understand your customer's problem across four layers. Most campaigns fail here — not at the tool level.

The 4 Layers of Customer Pain

  • Functional: What's broken, slow, or manual in their workflow?
  • Emotional: What frustrates them? What keeps them up at night?
  • Social: How do they look to their boss, board, or peers?
  • Financial: Where are they losing money or missing revenue?

The Workaround Test

Ask: "What's the worst workaround your target customer uses today?" If the answer is "Excel", "manually via email", "nothing", or "an intern does it" — you've found a real problem. The worse the workaround, the higher the willingness to pay.

Value Proposition Quick Check

Can you complete this bridge: Customer Pain → Your Pain Reliever? If you can't name a specific customer pain for each feature you plan to pitch — you lack clarity. No tool can fix that.

2
Relevance Over Personalization
Jordan Crawford Methodology
10 min

Most outbound guides say "personalize your emails." That advice produces emails like "I saw your LinkedIn post" — which proves you Googled their name, nothing more. Crawford's counter-thesis: Relevance beats personalization.

The Difference in Practice

Personalized (weak)

"Hi Thomas, I noticed you work at Company X and your team is growing. We help SaaS companies with lead generation..."

Relevant (strong)

"Thomas, you have 3 open SDR roles on your careers page. That means your outbound needs to scale — but new SDRs take 3-6 months to ramp. What if you could get the pipeline output of a 3-person SDR team in 2 weeks, without hiring anyone?"

Micro-Campaigns: Quality Over Volume

50 deeply researched accounts beat 5,000 generic contacts. A 50-account micro-campaign typically sees 8-15% reply rates vs. 1-2% for spray-and-pray. For seed-stage startups, you need 5-10 real conversations — not 75 "please unsubscribe" replies.

Opening — The Uncomfortable Truth

Most outbound campaigns don't fail because of bad tools or poor deliverability. They fail because the sender doesn't truly understand the customer's problem. They pitch features instead of addressing pain. They talk about their product instead of the customer's world.

The 4 Layers of Customer Pain (Maja Voje)

LayerQuestionExample (HR-Tech SaaS)

  • An email that only hits the functional layer ("We automate your recruiting") gets ignored
  • An email that hits emotional + financial ("You're losing 9,000/month on 3 open roles — and top candidates go to competitors because your process is too slow") gets replies
  • The difference: describing features vs. mirroring the pain the customer actually feels

The Workaround Test

Ask: "What's the worst workaround your target customer uses today?" If the answer is "Excel spreadsheet", "manually by email", "nothing", or "an intern handles it" — you've found a real problem. The worse the current workaround, the higher the willingness to pay.

Value Proposition Canvas — The Quick Check

Before outbound makes sense, you need to build this bridge:

Customer Profile

Jobs: What are they trying to accomplish?
Pains: What makes those jobs hard?
Gains: What outcome would delight them?

Your Value Map

Products: What do you offer?
Pain Relievers: How do you reduce specific pains?
Gain Creators: How do you create specific gains?

If you can't name a specific customer pain for each pain reliever — you lack clarity. No tool can fix that.

GTM Power Hour — The 3-Sentence Test

Can you answer each of these in one sentence?

  • Who exactly is your customer? (Not "all B2B companies")
  • What problem do you solve? (In customer language, not feature language)
  • Why you vs. alternatives? (Including "do nothing")

If you can't → research is your next step, not campaigns.

Relevance Over Personalization
Jordan Crawford / Blueprint Methodology
10 min

The Crawford Principle — The Most Important Mindset Shift

Most outbound guides say: "Personalize your emails." That sounds right, but it produces emails like:

Personalized (weak)

"Hi Thomas, I saw you work at Company X and your LinkedIn post about AI was really interesting. I wanted to introduce myself..."

That's personalization. The recipient thinks: "They Googled my name. So what."

Crawford's counter-position: Relevance > Personalization. Relevance means you understand the person's business problem. You know what's happening inside their company right now. You write an email that could only have been sent to this one person.

Relevant (strong)

"Thomas, you have 3 open SDR roles on your careers page. That means your outbound needs to scale — but new SDRs take 3-6 months to become productive. What if you could get the pipeline results of a 3-person SDR team in 2 weeks, without hiring anyone?"

The difference: The second email proves you researched the company and understand what's happening there right now. You're talking about their problem, not your product.

Micro-Campaigns — Quality Over Volume

Crawford's rule: 50 deeply researched accounts are worth more than 5,000 generic contacts.

  • 5,000-contact campaigns force generic messaging ("one size fits all")
  • 50-contact campaigns allow account-specific research
  • Reply rate for a 50-account micro-campaign: typically 8-15%
  • Reply rate for a 5,000-contact spray-and-pray campaign: 1-2%
  • 50 x 12% = 6 real conversations. That's all a seed-stage startup needs.

AI Research Agent in Clay

The most powerful feature for micro-campaigns: let an AI agent (Claude/GPT) run a mini-analysis per account:

  • Input: Website, job postings, company data
  • Prompt: "What's the most likely problem this company has in the area of [your topic]? Formulate a hypothesis in 2 sentences."
  • Output: An account-specific hypothesis you can directly embed in the email

This is where automation and relevance converge. You're no longer writing generically, but you're also not spending 30 minutes per account on manual research.

C
Pain-First Copywriting
The email structure that gets replies
7 min

The Framework: Problem → Implication → Solution → Soft CTA

Most cold emails follow this pattern: "We are Company X. We do Y. Our features are Z. Want to chat?" That's a pitch. Nobody replies to pitches from strangers.

Instead — the Pain-First Framework:

1
Name the Problem (Sentence 1)

Show you understand what's happening at their company. Use data from Clay.
"You have 3 open SDR roles that have been posted for 6 weeks."

2
Show the Implication (Sentence 2)

What does this problem mean for them? Make the pain concrete.
"That means your pipeline isn't growing, while your burn rate keeps running."

3
Hint at the Solution (Sentence 3)

No feature list. Only the outcome.
"We helped [similar company] build the pipeline of a 3-person SDR team in 3 weeks — without hiring anyone."

4
Soft CTA (Sentence 4)

Not "Can we jump on a call?" — that's too much commitment.
"Would it make sense for me to show you how that looks?"

Total email: 4 sentences. Under 80 words. No attachments. No HTML.

3 Copywriting Rules for Founders

  • Write like a human, not a marketer. No buzzwords, no corporate-speak. Imagine you're writing a text to an acquaintance.
  • One idea per email. Don't cram three value props into one message. One hypothesis, one pain, one question.
  • Subject lines: short, lowercase, specific. "quick question about your sales team" works better than "Revolutionize Your Sales Pipeline with AI-Powered Automation".

The Seed-Stage Stack

↓

That's it. No Salesloft, no Outreach, no 6-tool stack. Cost: ~$200-300/month.

Technical Basics You Can't Skip

  • Burner domains: Never send from your main domain. A blocked domain = all your email traffic is compromised. Buy 2-3 similar domains.
  • Warm-up: New domains need 2-3 weeks of warm-up before you can send. Instantly handles this automatically. Don't skip it.
  • Volume: Start with max 25-30 emails per day per account. Not 200 on day one.
  • Verification: Verify every email address before sending. Bounce rate above 3% = domain reputation destroyed.

Mission-Critical Filter (Maja Voje)

For every activity, every new tool, every new tactic — ask 3 questions:

  • Does this directly move me toward my next milestone?
  • Is there evidence this works in my specific market?
  • Can I execute this well with my current resources?

If not 3/3 → it's a distraction, not a priority.
